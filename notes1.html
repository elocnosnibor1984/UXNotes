Subject: Youtube Main Page

I'm surprised I never noticed the top buttons to change between Home, Trending and Subscription before.

I'm also a little surprised by the hidden sidebar menu - maybe people don't use it enough to justify having it visible all the time? I don't know that I've ever used it.

The style is so simple - light grey lines, off white background, a series of videos and a short description ... I guess I imagine that sites that are this huge would be overflowing with features. The magic is that it gets right to showing you videos that you want to watch. It really just pushes content. If it can give you videos that you want to watch then it can show you ads and make money.

Again with the simple header - SiteID, Search bar, upload, alert (icon), personalized google icon (so that you share their brand across their platforms - saving them space and geting buy-in in the process). The sub navbar with just 3 choices is interesting though - I wouldn't have thought to do that. I suppose that functionality is important enough to display it so prominently?

The lesson of the day is to be good at giving the user what they want - and they might not want a lot of bells and whistles.

Subject: Apple ios article

UI is tied to screen size. As the screen size changes, so must the UI because you can't have as many options on mobile. Text size is also impacted by mobile design - needs to stay readable.

There needs to be significant contrast between font color and background so that it is readable - even for people with color blindness or other visual impairments.

Image distortion - images shouldn't be allowed to become difficult to view because of the changing screen size.

Layouts should be consistent and easy to use. Example: the use of color and the bar for a password's strength on registration.

Essentially a 3 column layout where main article gets 2 columns at the top. Gives links to the "big" stories on the left, with "bigger" stories on the right. The navbar is interesting too: white letters on black background - the only color is the CNN Site ID, which is also highlighted by the way it extends below the navbar. How do you do that?

Subject: Quora Main Page

Only red color is SiteID and "read" with icon -presumably these are the major focuses of the site.

Only the center column moves - keeps functionality on page at all times. Right column is to encourage user interaction with the site in the form of goals for the user to complete (with one always selected - visit your feed). Left column is to help you get to more interesting content? Things you've bookmarked? The left column strikes me as something that could be more clearly defined. I'm not sure what it's purpose is.

Pop up menu gives options related to the posts - covers up other content while clicked, post background goes grey-blue when mouse over.

Content itself is mostly hidden - just teases the user so they can quickly scan what is most interesting - get to more content faster.

On click - article becomes modal. Preserves one page app.

Factors to consider when reviewing the UX/UI of a site:

  • What do I want to do on the site?
  • What does the site seem to think I want to do?
  • Is there anything that gets in my way?
  • Do the feature I want to use need to be displayed more prominently?
  • What is the purpose of each screen element/group of elements?
  • What is most -> least prominent on the site?
  • How do I know what is clickable? Any issues with this?
  • Is there too much on the page? Too little?
  • How busy is the art? Does it distract?
  • How wordy is the page?
  • Does this seem to be a deep or shallow hierarchy?
  • What form of navigation does the site use? (breadcrumbs, headings, active navbar elements, page names, etc)
  • Does the homepage communicate the identity & mission?

I appreciate how the site layout/design is so simple and the pictures are so clear & sharp - really puts the focus on the products - and they fill the page with products. They are trying to take you directly to things to buy.

Subject: Chase landing page

The first pane's main focus is login, followed by pushing some credit card info. The second pane's main focus is "Choose what's right for you". I really like framing the services in this way - very customer focused. It's a list of all the services that chase offers. The second half of the second pane are helpful articles meant to give users information about topics like things to consider when buying a home, making financial goals, and your credit score. These are good ways to build goodwill with your users - it shows that you are there to help them, even for free. The third pane "News and Stories" follows in a similar vein, but also has "articles" about famous people using their products. I suppose this is a way to build an image of prestige - famous, admirable people admiring your product.

Footer 1 presumably lists all of chase's services along with a short blurb and some links. Footer 2 contains other info about privacy, security, accessibility, site map, member of FDIC and equal opportunity housing lender.

I like the way the fixed navbar changes from "Chase" to just the logo, and transitions from transparent to semi transparent blue. It's a nice touch that adds to the sense that this is a very professional site.

Subject: Slate Main page

I never noticed before all the different ways they frame their articles. Main article is a jumbotron, next two are just boxes - one white background, one yellow, below that a jumbotron ad, a material card, and a bigger box article. Next is a thin add for Slate+, then a box listing other article headlines, and another jumbotron but with a box inset. Then two more boxes - a wide white box and an almost square purple one, then 3 articles without boxes broken up by pipes.

The sidebar on the right is capped by the Slate logo, then a link to see the most recent articles in black background with white text. The articles on the right all have the tight grey angled slashes that subtly distinguish them. The Slate cap moves down the page fixed in place.

One of the most clever things they do is shameless repeat the articles in a three column spread at the bottom: Most read, and Most Shared, broken up by a dark "Dear Prudence" column. I like how these are numbered. It gives the impression that there are more articles and I assume that people eventually decide to click on them as they scan the page - they see each article about twice.

All links change to slate purple on highlight when set on white background, and to yellow when set against a dark background.
